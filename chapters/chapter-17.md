# Chapter 17 — Śraddhātrayavibhāgayogaḥ (श्रद्धात्रयविभागयोगः)  

**The Yoga of the Distinction of the Three Kinds of Conviction** · 28 verses

---

### 17.1

अर्जुन उवाच ।  
ये शास्त्रविधिमुत्सृज्य यजन्ते श्रद्धयान्विताः ।  
तेषां निष्ठा तु का कृष्ण सत्त्वमाहो रजस्तमः ॥ १ ॥  

arjuna uvāca |  
ye śāstravidhim utsṛjya yajante śraddhayānvitāḥ |  
teṣāṃ niṣṭhā tu kā kṛṣṇa sattvam āho rajas tamaḥ || 1 ||  

Arjuna said:
Those who, having cast aside the injunctions of the scriptures, worship endowed with faith (*śraddhā*) — what is their position, Kṛṣṇa? Is it *sattva* (goodness), *rajas* (passion), or *tamas* (darkness)?

**Simple Translation:** Arjuna asked: Krishna, what is the state of those who ignore the formal rules of scriptures but still perform their work with faith? Is their nature pure (Sattva), passionate (Rajas), or dull (Tamas)?

**Contemporary Relevance:** Integrity over Rules. This is a very modern question: 'Can I be a good person and grow spiritually even if I don't follow a specific religion or set of rules?' Arjuna is asking if 'internal sincerity' (faith) is more important than 'external ritual.'

> **Note:** Arjuna poses a critical sociological and theological question. What happens to the person who possesses genuine spiritual sincerity (*śraddhā*) but does not follow orthodox scriptural rules?

---

### 17.2

श्रीभगवानुवाच ।  
त्रिविधा भवति श्रद्धा देहिनां सा स्वभावजा ।  
सात्त्विकी राजसी चैव तामसी चेति तां शृणु ॥ २ ॥  

śrībhagavān uvāca |  
trividhā bhavati śraddhā dehināṃ sā svabhāvajā |  
sāttvikī rājasī caiva tāmasī ceti tāṃ śṛṇu || 2 ||  

The Blessed Lord said:
The faith of embodied beings is of three kinds, born of their own inherent nature (*svabhāva*). It is *sāttvikī*, *rājasī*, and *tāmasī*. Hear of it.

**Simple Translation:** The Blessed Lord said: The faith of living beings is of three kinds, depending on their own inherent nature: it can be pure (Sattvic), passionate (Rajasic), or dull (Tamasic). Listen to the details.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Subjective Filter. Your 'faith'—what you believe in and how you commit—is not a random choice. It is a reflection of your current character (nature). Understanding this helps you see that to change what you believe, you must first change who you are.

---

### 17.3

सत्त्वानुरूपा सर्वस्य श्रद्धा भवति भारत ।  
श्रद्धामयोऽयं पुरुषो यो यच्छ्रद्धः स एव सः ॥ ३ ॥  

sattvānurūpā sarvasya śraddhā bhavati bhārata |  
śraddhāmayo 'yaṃ puruṣo yo yacchraddhaḥ sa eva saḥ || 3 ||  

The faith of everyone is according to his disposition, O Bhārata. Man is made of his faith; whatever his faith is, that indeed is he.

**Simple Translation:** Everyone's faith matches their own inner nature, Arjuna. A person is essentially what their faith is; as their faith is, so are they.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The First Principle of Identity. 'You are what you believe.' Your core convictions and the things you trust determine your actions, your personality, and your destiny. This is a call to audit your deepest beliefs, as they are the 'code' that runs your life.

---

### 17.4

यजन्ते सात्त्विका देवान्यक्षरक्षांसि राजसाः ।  
प्रेतान्भूतगणांश्चान्ये यजन्ते तामसा जनाः ॥ ४ ॥  

yajante sāttvikā devān yakṣarakṣāṃsi rājasāḥ |  
pretān bhūtagaṇāṃś cānye yajante tāmasā janāḥ || 4 ||  

Those of a *sāttvika* nature worship the gods; the *rājasika* worship the Yakṣas and Rākṣasas; and the others, the *tāmasika* people, worship ghosts and multitudes of spirits.

**Simple Translation:** Pure (Sattvic) people focus on the divine powers; passionate (Rajasic) people focus on power and wealth; and dull (Tamasic) people focus on ghosts and lower spirits.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Direction of Attention. What do you worship? If you 'worship' (focus on) excellence and truth, you become pure. If you worship money and status, you become stressed. If you worship drama and the past, you become dull. Your 'object of worship' is your future self.

---

### 17.5

अशास्त्रविहितं घोरं तप्यन्ते ये तपो जनाः ।  
दम्भाहङ्कारसंयुक्ताः कामरागबलान्विताः ॥ ५ ॥  

aśāstravihitaṃ ghoraṃ tapyante ye tapo janāḥ |  
dambhāhaṅkārasaṃyuktāḥ kāmarāgabalānvitāḥ || 5 ||  

Those men who perform severe austerities not enjoined by scripture, combined with hypocrisy and egoism, impelled by the force of desire and attachment;

**Simple Translation:** Some people perform severe and ego-driven austerities that are not recommended by wisdom, driven by hypocrisy, pride, desire, and attachment.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Trap of Extreme Ego. Doing 'hard things' just to show off or because you are obsessed with an outcome is not growth—it’s 'demonic' (ego-driven). This is a warning against extreme 'hustle culture' or 'performative suffering' that isn't rooted in wisdom.

---

### 17.6

कर्शयन्तः शरीरस्थं भूतग्राममचेतसः ।  
मां चैवान्तःशरीरस्थं तान्विद्ध्यासुरनिश्चयान् ॥ ६ ॥  

karśayantaḥ śarīrasthaṃ bhūtagrāmam acetasaḥ |  
māṃ caivāntaḥśarīrasthaṃ tān viddhy āsuraniścayān || 6 ||  

Senselessly tormenting the multitude of elements situated in the body, and also Me dwelling within the body — know them to be of demonic resolve.

**Simple Translation:** They senselessly torture their own bodies and the life-force within them—and even Me, who lives within. Know that their resolutions are destructive.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Body as a Temple. Torturing yourself through extreme diets, lack of sleep, or mental self-abuse is an act of violence against the Absolute reality living within you. Real discipline should nourish and refine the system, not destroy it.

> **Note:** A direct condemnation of extreme physical self-mortification. The divine is immanent within the body; therefore, to torture the body is to commit violence against the divine.

---

### 17.7

आहारस्त्वपि सर्वस्य त्रिविधो भवति प्रियः ।  
यज्ञस्तपस्तथा दानं तेषां भेदमिमं शृणु ॥ ७ ॥  

āhāras tv api sarvasya trividho bhavati priyaḥ |  
yajñas tapas tathā dānaṃ teṣāṃ bhedam imaṃ śṛṇu || 7 ||  

Even the food that is dear to all is of three kinds. So are sacrifice, austerity, and charity. Hear this distinction of them.

**Simple Translation:** Even the food people prefer, and their ways of contributing, practicing discipline, and giving, are of three kinds. Listen to the difference between them.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Lifestyle Audit. Character is not just 'philosophy'; it shows up in your 'grocery list' and your 'habits.' Krishna is about to show how the three Gunas (qualities) affect every mundane detail of your daily life.

---

### 17.8

आयुःसत्त्वबलारोग्यसुखप्रीतिविवर्धनाः ।  
रस्याः स्निग्धाः स्थिरा हृद्या आहाराः सात्त्विकप्रियाः ॥ ८ ॥  

āyuḥsattvabalārogyasukhaprītivivardhanāḥ |  
rasyāḥ snigdhāḥ sthirā hṛdyā āhārāḥ sāttvikapriyāḥ || 8 ||  

Foods that promote longevity, vitality, strength, health, happiness, and satisfaction, and that are juicy, soothing, substantial, and agreeable, are dear to the *sāttvika*.

**Simple Translation:** Foods that increase life, energy, strength, health, happiness, and joy—foods that are juicy, smooth, substantial, and pleasant—are loved by the pure (Sattvic).

**Contemporary Relevance:** High-Performance Fuel. This is the First Principle of Nutrition: eat things that build you up over the long term. Sattvic food is 'Life-affirming' (Ayuh-vardhana). If what you eat makes you feel clean, energetic, and happy, you are fueling your growth.

---

### 17.9

कट्वम्ललवणात्युष्णतीक्ष्णरूक्षविदाहिनः ।  
आहारा राजसस्येष्टा दुःखशोकामयप्रदाः ॥ ९ ॥  

kaṭvamlalavaṇātyuṣṇatīkṣṇarūkṣavidāhinaḥ |  
āhārā rājasasyeṣṭā duḥkhaśokāmayapradāḥ || 9 ||  

Foods that are excessively bitter, sour, salty, hot, pungent, dry, and burning, which cause pain, grief, and disease, are desired by the *rājasika*.

**Simple Translation:** Foods that are too bitter, sour, salty, hot, pungent, dry, or burning—which cause pain, grief, and disease—are preferred by the passionate (Rajasic).

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Energy of Over-Stimulation. Passionate people love 'extreme' flavors that give a quick 'kick' but lead to a 'crash' or health problems later. This mirrors a life of 'extreme highs and lows' that eventually causes burnout and suffering.

---

### 17.10

यातयामं गतरसं पूति पर्युषितं च यत् ।  
उच्छिष्टमपि चामेध्यं भोजनं तामसप्रियम् ॥ १० ॥  

yātayāmaṃ gatarasaṃ pūti paryuṣitaṃ ca yat |  
ucchiṣṭam api cāmedhyaṃ bhojanaṃ tāmasapriyam || 10 ||  

Food that is stale, tasteless, putrid, left overnight, and also leftover scraps and impure things, is the food dear to the *tāmasika*.

**Simple Translation:** Food that is stale, tasteless, smelly, left overnight, or impure, is what the dull (Tamasic) people love.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Energy of Decay. Stale and low-quality food leads to a stale and low-quality mind. If you don't care about the quality of what you consume, you are fueling your own laziness and confusion. This is a call to 'conscious eating' as a path to clarity.

---

### 17.11

अफलाकाङ्क्षिभिर्यज्ञो विधिदृष्टो य इज्यते ।  
यष्टव्यमेवेति मनः समाधाय स सात्त्विकः ॥ ११ ॥  

aphalākāṅkṣibhir yajño vidhidṛṣṭo ya ijyate |  
yaṣṭavyam eveti manaḥ samādhāya sa sāttvikaḥ || 11 ||  

That sacrifice which is offered by those not desiring the fruit, according to the scriptural injunction, with the mind firmly fixed on the thought "it is a duty to sacrifice," is *sāttvika*.

**Simple Translation:** Work (sacrifice) that is done because it is a duty, without wanting any reward and with a focused mind, is pure (Sattvic).

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Standard of Professionalism. This is the highest level of execution: doing something simply because it *needs* to be done and it is 'right.' When you remove the 'What's in it for me?' motivation, your work becomes clean, powerful, and peaceful.

---

### 17.12

अभिसन्धाय तु फलं दम्भार्थमपि चैव यत् ।  
इज्यते भरतश्रेष्ठ तं यज्ञं विद्धि राजसम् ॥ १२ ॥  

abhisandhāya tu phalaṃ dambhārtham api caiva yat |  
ijyate bharataśreṣṭha taṃ yajñaṃ viddhi rājasam || 12 ||  

But that sacrifice which is offered aiming at the fruit, and also merely for the sake of hypocrisy — know that sacrifice, O best of the Bharatas, to be *rājasika*.

**Simple Translation:** But work that is done only for show or with an eye on the reward, Arjuna, know that to be passionate (Rajasic).

**Contemporary Relevance:** Transactional Living. If you are only working for the 'bonus' or the 'praise' (dambha), you are in a state of Rajas. This makes you vulnerable to stress because your happiness is tied to a result you can't always control.

---

### 17.13

विधिहीनमसृष्टान्नं मन्त्रहीनमदक्षिणम् ।  
श्रद्धाविरहितं यज्ञं तामसं परिचक्षते ॥ १३ ॥  

vidhihīnam asṛṣṭānnaṃ mantrahīnam adakṣiṇam |  
śraddhāvirahitaṃ yajñaṃ tāmasaṃ paricakṣate || 13 ||  

The sacrifice devoid of scriptural injunctions, in which no food is distributed, devoid of *mantras*, devoid of the priest's fee, and lacking faith — they declare to be *tāmasika*.

**Simple Translation:** Work that ignores rules, shares nothing with others, has no wisdom, no integrity, and no faith, is called dull (Tamasic).

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Low-Bar Existence. This is work done with 'zero effort' and 'zero care.' It’s the energy of 'just getting by.' This kind of action builds nothing and only deepens your own confusion and lack of purpose.

---

### 17.14

देवद्विजगुरुप्राज्ञपूजनं शौचमार्जवम् ।  
ब्रह्मचर्यमहिंसा च शारीरं तप उच्यते ॥ १४ ॥  

devadvijaguruprājñapūjanaṃ śaucam ārjavam |  
brahmacaryam ahiṃsā ca śārīraṃ tapa ucyate || 14 ||  

Worship of the gods, the twice-born (brahmins), teachers, and the wise; purity, rectitude, celibacy, and non-violence — this is called austerity of the body.

**Simple Translation:** Respecting the wise and the teachers, being pure, honest, self-controlled, and non-violent—this is called the discipline (tapas) of the body.

**Contemporary Relevance:** Physical Integrity. Self-discipline starts with the 'hardware.' How you treat others, how clean you are, and how you control your physical impulses are the foundation of all other types of growth. It’s about being a 'well-regulated' human being.

---

### 17.15

अनुद्वेगकरं वाक्यं सत्यं प्रियहितं च यत् ।  
स्वाध्यायाभ्यसनं चैव वाङ्मयं तप उच्यते ॥ १५ ॥  

anudvegakaraṃ vākyaṃ satyaṃ priyahitaṃ ca yat |  
svādhyāyābhyasanaṃ caiva vāṅmayaṃ tapa ucyate || 15 ||  

Speech that causes no agitation, that is true, pleasant, and beneficial, and the practice of reciting the Veda — this is called austerity of speech.

**Simple Translation:** Speaking words that don't cause stress, that are true, pleasant, and helpful—and the regular study of wisdom—is called the discipline of speech.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Ethics of Communication. This is a brilliant First Principle for the modern age: 1. Don't cause stress (anudvega). 2. Be true (satya). 3. Be pleasant (priya). 4. Be helpful (hita). If your communication follows these four rules, you become a master of influence and peace.

---

### 17.16

मनःप्रसादः सौम्यत्वं मौनमात्मविनिग्रहः ।  
भावसंशुद्धिरित्येतत्तपो मानसमुच्यते ॥ १६ ॥  

manaḥprasādaḥ saumyatvaṃ maunam ātmavinigrahaḥ |  
bhāvasaṃśuddhir ityetat tapo mānasam ucyate || 16 ||  

Serenity of mind, gentleness, silence, self-restraint, and purity of existence — this is called austerity of the mind.

**Simple Translation:** Mental peace, gentleness, silence, self-restraint, and purity of intent—this is called the discipline of the mind.

**Contemporary Relevance:** Internal Sovereignty. The 'Silence' (Mauna) mentioned here is the most powerful: the ability to stop the 'internal noise' of the mind. Purity of intent (bhava-samshuddhih) means having no hidden agendas. This is the peak of psychological health.

> **Note:** "Silence" (*mauna*) here is placed under austerity of the *mind*, not speech. True silence is the cessation of internal mental chatter, not just the physical withholding of words.

---

### 17.17

श्रद्धया परया तप्तं तपस्तत्त्रिविधं नरैः ।  
अफलाकाङ्क्षिभिर्युक्तैः सात्त्विकं परिचक्षते ॥ १७ ॥  

śraddhayā parayā taptaṃ tapas tat trividhaṃ naraiḥ |  
aphalākāṅkṣibhir yuktaiḥ sāttvikaṃ paricakṣate || 17 ||  

This threefold austerity, practiced with supreme faith by men who do not desire the fruit and who are disciplined, they declare to be *sāttvika*.

**Simple Translation:** This threefold discipline (body, speech, mind), when practiced with total trust by people who don't want rewards, is called pure (Sattvic).

**Contemporary Relevance:** Unified Growth. When you align your body, your words, and your thoughts toward a higher purpose—without being greedy for results—you achieve a state of 'Sattva.' This is the state of 'Total Integrity' where you are finally 'whole.'

---

### 17.18

सत्कारमानपूजार्थं तपो दम्भेन चैव यत् ।  
क्रियते तदिह प्रोक्तं राजसं चलमध्रुवम् ॥ १८ ॥  

satkāramānapūjārthaṃ tapo dambhena caiva yat |  
kriyate tad iha proktaṃ rājasaṃ calam adhruvam || 18 ||  

That austerity which is performed for the sake of obtaining respect, honor, and reverence, and with hypocrisy, is declared here to be *rājasika*, being unstable and impermanent.

**Simple Translation:** Discipline that is done just to get respect, honor, or praise—and is done with hypocrisy—is passionate (Rajasic). It is unstable and doesn't last.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Flimsiness of Pride. If you are only 'disciplined' when people are watching, your growth is a lie. This 'performative' discipline is 'unstable' (calam) because it depends on the opinions of others. Real growth must be internal.

---

### 17.19

मूढग्राहेणात्मनो यत्पीडया क्रियते तपः ।  
परस्योत्सादनार्थं वा तत्तामसमुदाहृतम् ॥ १९ ॥  

mūḍhagrāheṇātmano yat pīḍayā kriyate tapaḥ |  
parasyotsādanārthaṃ vā tat tāmasam udāhṛtam || 19 ||  

That austerity which is performed with deluded understanding, by torturing oneself, or for the purpose of destroying another, is declared to be *tāmasika*.

**Simple Translation:** Discipline done with a foolish intent, by hurting oneself or trying to destroy others, is called dull (Tamasic).

**Contemporary Relevance:** Misguided Suffering. Some people think that 'suffering' itself is spiritual. Krishna clarifies that hurting yourself or others is just 'Tamas' (confusion). Real discipline should lead to 'Shreyas' (the good), not to pain or destruction.

---

### 17.20

दातव्यमिति यद्दानं दीयतेऽनुपकारिणे ।  
देशे काले च पात्रे च तद्दानं सात्त्विकं स्मृतम् ॥ २० ॥  

dātavyam iti yad dānaṃ dīyate 'nupakāriṇe |  
deśe kāle ca pātre ca tad dānaṃ sāttvikaṃ smṛtam || 20 ||  

That gift which is given with the thought "it must be given," to one who does nothing in return, in the proper place and time, and to a worthy recipient, that gift is remembered as *sāttvika*.

**Simple Translation:** A gift given because 'it is right to give,' at the right time and place, to a worthy person who can do nothing in return, is a pure (Sattvic) gift.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The First Principle of Charity. True giving is not a 'trade.' It is an act of acknowledging your responsibility to the system. By giving to someone who *cannot* pay you back, you are practicing 'Pure Contribution' and breaking the ego's 'transactional' habit.

---

### 17.21

यत्तु प्रत्युपकारार्थं फलमुद्दिश्य वा पुनः ।  
दीयते च परिक्लिष्टं तद्दानं राजसं स्मृतम् ॥ २१ ॥  

yat tu pratyupakārārthaṃ phalam uddiśya vā punaḥ |  
dīyate ca parikliṣṭaṃ tad dānaṃ rājasaṃ smṛtam || 21 ||  

But that which is given for the sake of a return favor, or with an eye on the fruit, or is given grudgingly — that gift is remembered as *rājasika*.

**Simple Translation:** But a gift given with the hope of getting something back, or for a specific result, or given with a heavy heart, is a passionate (Rajasic) gift.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The 'Strings-Attached' Gift. If you feel 'pain' (pariklishtam) when you give, or if you are calculating what you will get in return, you haven't really 'given' anything—you’ve just made an 'investment.' This doesn't purify the mind; it only increases the ego's 'thirst.'

---

### 17.22

अदेशकाले यद्दानमपात्रेभ्यश्च दीयते ।  
असत्कृतमवज्ञातं तत्तामसमुदाहृतम् ॥ २२ ॥  

adeśakāle yad dānam apātrebhyaś ca dīyate |  
asatkṛtam avajñātaṃ tat tāmasam udāhṛtam || 22 ||  

That gift which is given at the wrong place and time, and to unworthy recipients, without respect and with contempt, is declared to be *tāmasika*.

**Simple Translation:** A gift given at the wrong time or place, to an unworthy person, or given with disrespect and contempt, is a dull (Tamasic) gift.

**Contemporary Relevance:** Irresponsible Giving. Giving without 'thinking' or giving in a way that insults the receiver is 'Tamasic.' True contribution requires 'Discernment' (Vidhi). You must ensure that your resources are actually helping and not causing more harm or ego.

---

### 17.23

ॐ तत्सदिति निर्देशो ब्रह्मणस्त्रिविधः स्मृतः ।  
ब्राह्मणास्तेन वेदाश्च यज्ञाश्च विहिताः पुरा ॥ २३ ॥  

oṃ tat sad iti nirdeśo brahmaṇas trividhaḥ smṛtaḥ |  
brāhmaṇās tena vedāś ca yajñāś ca vihitāḥ purā || 23 ||  

"Oṃ, Tat, Sat" — this is remembered as the threefold designation of Brahman. By this were the brahmins, the Vedas, and the sacrifices ordained in ancient times.

**Simple Translation:** 'OM TAT SAT' is the threefold name of the Absolute. Since ancient times, it has been used to validate teachers, wisdom, and selfless work.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Universal Validation Formula. 1. OM (The Source). 2. TAT (The Detachment—'It is That, not Mine'). 3. SAT (The Reality/Goodness). This formula is a 'reset' for any action. It reminds us to start with the Source, act without ego, and ensure the act is Good.

> **Note:** A profound linguistic formula. *Oṃ* invokes the absolute sound; *Tat* ("That") refers to the transcendent reality beyond personal attachment; *Sat* ("Being" or "Good") grounds the action in objective reality and ethical purity.

---

### 17.24

तस्मादोमित्युदाहृत्य यज्ञदानतपःक्रियाः ।  
प्रवर्तन्ते विधानोक्ताः सततं ब्रह्मवादिनाम् ॥ २४ ॥  

tasmād om ity udāhṛtya yajñadānatapaḥkriyāḥ |  
pravartante vidhānoktāḥ satataṃ brahmavādinām || 24 ||  

Therefore, having uttered "Oṃ," the acts of sacrifice, charity, and austerity, as prescribed by the injunctions, are always begun by the expounders of Brahman.

**Simple Translation:** Therefore, seekers of the Absolute always start their work, charity, and discipline by saying 'OM' as the rules recommend.

**Contemporary Relevance:** Starting with the 'Why'. Saying 'OM' is a way of 'tagging' your action as purposeful and connected to the universal source. It’s like setting the 'context' for a project before you begin, ensuring you don't get lost in the 'how' and forget the 'why.'

---

### 17.25

तदित्यनभिसन्धाय फलं यज्ञतपःक्रियाः ।  
दानक्रियाश्च विविधाः क्रियन्ते मोक्षकाङ्क्षिभिः ॥ २५ ॥  

tad ityanabhisandhāya phalaṃ yajñatapaḥkriyāḥ |  
dānakriyāś ca vividhāḥ kriyante mokṣakāṅkṣibhiḥ || 25 ||  

With "Tat" (That), not aiming at the fruit, the various acts of sacrifice, austerity, and acts of charity are performed by those desiring liberation.

**Simple Translation:** By saying 'TAT' (That), those who want freedom perform their work, discipline, and charity without wanting any personal reward.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Power of De-Personalization. 'TAT' means 'It is That.' It’s a mental trick to remove your 'I' from the work. When you say 'This work is TAT (the Absolute's),' you stop taking the success or failure personally. You act with the freedom of a true professional.

---

### 17.26

सद्भावे साधुभावे च सदित्येतत्प्रयुज्यते ।  
प्रशस्ते कर्मणि तथा सच्छब्दः पार्थ युज्यते ॥ २६ ॥  

sadbhāve sādhubhāve ca sad ity etat prayujyate |  
praśaste karmaṇi tathā sacchabdaḥ pārtha yujyate || 26 ||  

The word "Sat" is used in the sense of existence and in the sense of goodness. Likewise, O Pārtha, the word "Sat" is applied to an auspicious action.

**Simple Translation:** The word 'SAT' is used to mean 'Existence' and 'Goodness.' Arjuna, it is also used to describe an excellent and auspicious action.

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Definition of the 'Real'. In this philosophy, only what is 'Good' (Sat) is considered 'Real' or 'Existent.' This is a powerful ethical First Principle: an action is only truly 'valid' if it is both factual (existence) and moral (goodness).

---

### 17.27

यज्ञे तपसि दाने च स्थितिः सदिति चोच्यते ।  
कर्म चैव तदर्थीयं सदित्येवाभिधीयते ॥ २७ ॥  

yajñe tapasi dāne ca sthitiḥ sad iti cocyate |  
karma caiva tadarthīyaṃ sad ity evābhidhīyate || 27 ||  

And steadfastness in sacrifice, austerity, and charity is also called "Sat." And indeed, action performed for the sake of That is called "Sat."

**Simple Translation:** Steadiness in work, discipline, and charity is also called 'SAT.' And any action done for that ultimate purpose is also called 'SAT.'

**Contemporary Relevance:** The Value of Consistency. 'SAT' is not just a one-time event; it is 'Sthiti' (steadiness). Being consistently good and consistently disciplined is what makes your life 'Real' and 'Significant.' Consistency is the proof of integrity.

---

### 17.28

अश्रद्धया हुतं दत्तं तपस्तप्तं कृतं च यत् ।  
असदित्युच्यते पार्थ न च तत्प्रेत्य नो इह ॥ २८ ॥  

aśraddhayā hutaṃ dattaṃ tapas taptaṃ kṛtaṃ ca yat |  
asad ity ucyate pārtha na ca tat pretya no iha || 28 ||  

Whatever is offered in the fire, given, or performed as austerity, or whatever is done without faith, is called "Asat" (non-being), O Pārtha. It is of no value here or hereafter.

**Simple Translation:** Whatever is offered, given, or practiced without faith is called 'ASAT' (Non-existent), Arjuna. It has no value in this life or the next.

**Contemporary Relevance:** Faith as the 'Multiplier'. You can do all the 'right' things, but if you don't have 'Internal Sincerity' (Shraddha), your work is 'ASAT' (zero/nothing). Faith is the '1' that makes all the zeros of your actions count. Without a sincere heart, even perfect work is hollow.

> **Note:** The chapter concludes by answering Arjuna's opening question. Even technically correct actions are completely nullified ("Asat") if performed without the internal disposition of faith (*śraddhā*).
