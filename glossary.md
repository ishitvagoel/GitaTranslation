# Glossary of Key Sanskrit Terms

This glossary documents the principal Sanskrit terms encountered in the Bhagavad Gītā and the translation choices made for each occurrence. Because this translation uses context-sensitive rendering rather than fixed equivalents, the same term may be rendered differently in different verses. This glossary tracks those choices and their rationale.

---

## ātman (आत्मन्)
**Range of meaning:** self, the self, oneself, essential nature, spirit, soul
**Translation approach:** Rendered according to context. In philosophical passages about the imperishable self, kept as "the self" or "ātman." In reflexive usage, rendered as "oneself" or simply as a reflexive pronoun.

## bhakti (भक्ति)
**Range of meaning:** devotion, love, participation, loyalty
**Translation approach:** Usually rendered as "devotion." In the later chapters, it signifies the supreme spiritual path of loving surrender to the personal deity.

## brahman (ब्रह्मन्)
**Range of meaning:** the absolute, the expansive principle, the Vedas, sacred utterance, the ultimate reality
**Translation approach:** Context-dependent. Distinguished carefully from *Brahmā* (the creator deity) and *brāhmaṇa* (a member of the priestly class). Also used occasionally (e.g., Chapter 14) to refer to the great material womb (*mahat-prakṛti*).

## buddhi (बुद्धि)
**Range of meaning:** discernment, understanding, intellect, resolute intelligence, determination
**Translation approach:** Varies by context between "discernment," "understanding," and "resolve."

## dharma (धर्म)
**Range of meaning:** duty, law, right action, moral order, nature, essential function, righteousness
**Translation approach:** No single English word captures *dharma*. Rendered variously as "duty," "right action," "law," "moral order," or "essential nature" depending on context. In some cases left as *dharma* with a note.

## guṇa (गुण)
**Range of meaning:** quality, strand, constituent, attribute, rope
**Translation approach:** In the context of *prakṛti*'s three constituents (*sattva*, *rajas*, *tamas*), rendered as "strand" or "constituent quality." The three guṇas are not merely "qualities" but active forces that bind.

## karma / karman (कर्मन्)
**Range of meaning:** action, deed, work, ritual action, accumulated consequence of action
**Translation approach:** Context-dependent. "Action" in most philosophical contexts. "Deed" or "work" where the emphasis is practical. "Ritual action" where Vedic sacrifice is the referent.

## kṣetra / kṣetrajña (क्षेत्र / क्षेत्रज्ञ)
**Range of meaning:** field / knower of the field
**Translation approach:** Rendered literally as "field" (the body, the entirety of material and psychological phenomena) and "knower of the field" (the witnessing consciousness, the soul). 

## māyā (माया)
**Range of meaning:** illusion, creative power, magic, divine artifice
**Translation approach:** Rendered as "creative illusion" or "mystic power." It denotes the power by which the absolute manifests the phenomenal world, which is ultimately impermanent and deceptive.

## prakṛti (प्रकृति)
**Range of meaning:** nature, material nature, primal matter, the manifest world, one's own nature
**Translation approach:** "Material nature" or "nature" in Sāṅkhya-influenced passages. "One's own nature" when referring to individual constitution (*svabhāva*-adjacent usage).

## puruṣa (पुरुष)
**Range of meaning:** person, spirit, the conscious principle, the cosmic person
**Translation approach:** "The conscious principle" or "spirit" in Sāṅkhya contexts. "Person" in non-technical usage. *Puruṣottama* translates as the "Supreme Person."

## saṃnyāsa / tyāga (संन्यास / त्याग)
**Range of meaning:** renunciation / abandonment
**Translation approach:** Translated consistently to highlight the distinction made in Chapter 18. *Saṃnyāsa* (renunciation) refers to giving up action itself (or action born of desire), while *tyāga* (abandonment) refers to giving up the *fruit* of action.

## śraddhā (श्रद्धा)
**Range of meaning:** faith, conviction, trust, sincerity
**Translation approach:** Rendered as "faith" or "conviction." It is not mere belief in dogma, but an existential orientation and sincerity of purpose (explored deeply in Chapter 17).

## yajña (यज्ञ)
**Range of meaning:** sacrifice, ritual offering, worship
**Translation approach:** Translated as "sacrifice." The Gītā expands its meaning from strict Vedic fire rituals to encompass any dedicated action, including the "sacrifice of knowledge" and self-restraint.

## yoga (योग)
**Range of meaning:** discipline, practice, yoking, union, method, steadiness, equanimity
**Translation approach:** Highly context-sensitive. "Discipline" in many chapter titles and general usage. "Equanimity" where 2.48's definition is operative. "Practice" where method is emphasized. Sometimes left as *yoga* when no single English word suffices.
